# SOUBH Securities Pvt. Ltd. (Demo Company)

> **Document purpose:** This file defines the fictional company used as the baseline organization for the RICE MVP. It is intentionally detailed so the compliance workflow has a realistic company context before any real SEBI circular is uploaded.  
> **Important note:** SOUBH Securities Pvt. Ltd. is a **demo-only fictional company**. It is not a real regulated entity. The details below are designed to feel realistic enough for product demos, testing, and hackathon judging.

---

## 1. Company Identity

**Company Name:** SOUBH Securities Pvt. Ltd.  
**Common Short Name:** SOUBH Securities  
**Category:** Mid-sized retail stock broking firm  
**Business Type:** SEBI-registered stock broker (demo profile)  
**Head Office:** Mumbai, Maharashtra, India  
**Primary Market:** Indian retail securities market  
**Business Focus:** Online trading, demat services, client onboarding, investor support, and market access for retail customers

SOUBH Securities Pvt. Ltd. is presented as a modern, technology-driven brokerage company that serves retail investors across India. The company is designed to feel like a real mid-sized financial services institution with enough complexity to make compliance meaningful, but not so large that the demo becomes hard to understand.

The company has a strong digital-first identity. Most of its customers interact with the firm through a web trading platform, a mobile app, and customer support channels. The firm also maintains a back-office operations team, a compliance team, a security and IT function, and a small set of branch and partner offices for customer onboarding and relationship support.

For the purposes of the RICE MVP, this company acts as the **existing compliance baseline**. That means the system should already know what policies, records, tasks, approvals, and evidence exist inside SOUBH Securities before a new SEBI circular is uploaded. In a real deployment, this baseline would be built from the company’s documents and historical compliance actions. In the demo, it is pre-seeded so the system can immediately compare a new regulation against existing practice.

---

## 2. Company Mission

SOUBH Securities exists to provide easy, safe, and transparent access to Indian capital markets for retail investors. The company’s mission is to reduce friction in investing while maintaining a strong control environment around identity verification, order handling, reporting, risk management, customer communication, and regulatory compliance.

The internal philosophy of the company is simple: growth should never come at the cost of trust. That means the company wants to make investing smooth for first-time users and active traders, but it also wants to make sure controls are in place for KYC, grievance handling, trading safeguards, records, cybersecurity, and client communication.

This mission is important because it creates the right kind of tension for the demo. A brokerage firm is constantly balancing speed, customer experience, and regulatory discipline. That makes it the perfect setting for a compliance product like RICE.

---

## 3. What the Company Does

SOUBH Securities is a full-service retail brokerage firm. It does not try to act like a large institutional investment bank. Instead, it focuses on services commonly offered to individual investors and small business clients.

### Core services
- Equity trading
- Intraday trading
- Futures and options trading
- Demat account opening and maintenance
- Mutual fund distribution through digital channels
- IPO application support
- Portfolio statements and contract notes
- Research summaries and market insights
- Customer support for order and account issues

### Secondary services
- Client education and onboarding assistance
- Digital verification and account activation
- Margin monitoring and account alerts
- Trade confirmations and reporting
- Document storage and retrieval
- Complaint handling and escalation support

The company is not designed as a wealth manager for ultra-high-net-worth clients. It is designed as a broad retail brokerage platform serving regular investors. This matters because the compliance environment is a little more operationally demanding than a simple app, but still narrow enough to model in an MVP.

## 4. Business Model

SOUBH Securities earns money through a mix of brokerage fees, transaction-related charges, subscription products, and cross-sell services.

### Main revenue streams
1. **Brokerage charges**  
   Revenue from executed trades in equity and derivatives.

2. **Platform and service fees**  
   Small charges for account maintenance, premium tools, reports, or statement access.

3. **Margin-related income**  
   Income related to margin funding and related services, where applicable in the demo model.

4. **Distribution commissions**  
   Revenue from mutual fund or other permitted distribution activities.

5. **IPO and application support services**  
   Charges associated with IPO facilitation or client assistance where relevant.

6. **Value-added retail products**  
   Market analytics, alerts, premium support, and educational offerings.

The company’s business model is intentionally ordinary. It is not supposed to look like a unicorn. It is supposed to look believable. That gives the compliance system something real to work with: account onboarding, trade data, customer records, cyber controls, grievances, and policy maintenance.

## 5. Positioning in the Market

SOUBH Securities is positioned as a digitally enabled, trust-first brokerage that targets India’s growing base of retail investors. It aims to be fast like a fintech app but controlled like a regulated financial institution. The company wants to compete on simplicity, service quality, and reliability rather than aggressive discounting alone.

The company has three strategic promises:
- easy access to markets,
- reliable customer support,
- disciplined compliance.

These promises are useful in the demo because they explain why compliance matters to the business. A broker that mishandles records, communication, or client data can lose trust quickly. A compliance platform becomes valuable not only for avoiding penalties but for preserving the company’s operating reputation.

### Typical customer segments

- Options traders who want fast execution and reporting
- Small business owners who use the platform for market participation

### Customer expectations

- Clear and transparent reporting

The customer profile matters because it influences the type of compliance burden the company carries. A consumer-facing broker has to manage communication records, disclosures, order handling, client authorizations, grievance responses, and cybersecurity protections in a structured way.

## 9. Internal Culture

SOUBH Securities is described as a company with a pragmatic internal culture. The business cares about speed, but it also respects controls. Teams are encouraged to move quickly, yet they are expected to leave a paper trail for anything that affects customers, trading behavior, account data, or regulatory obligations.

### Cultural characteristics
- Customer-focused
- Process-aware
- Data-driven
- Compliance-conscious
- Moderately modern, not overly bureaucratic
- Comfortable with dashboards, tickets, and digital workflow
- Open to automation if it improves traceability

This culture is important because the RICE product should feel like a natural fit inside the company. The platform is not trying to force a strange new way of working onto a rigid organization. It is supporting an already process-oriented team that wants better visibility and less manual checking.

## 10. Leadership Structure

The leadership structure is intentionally simple and realistic.

### Executive roles
- **Managing Director / CEO**
- **Chief Operating Officer**
- **Chief Technology Officer**
- **Chief Compliance Officer**
- **Head of Customer Experience**
- **Head of Finance**
- **Head of Legal Support**

### Decision style
The company uses a centralized approval model for regulatory matters. The compliance officer or compliance head reviews obligations, assigns tasks to relevant departments, and escalates high-risk items to leadership if required. Minor operational changes may be handled by department heads directly, but anything related to policy updates, customer disclosures, incident management, or regulatory interpretation needs visibility from compliance.

This structure is important because RICE needs a place to map tasks and responsibilities. The company should not feel flat or unrealistic. There must be a chain of accountability.

---

## 11. Department Overview

SOUBH Securities has a limited but practical departmental structure.

### A. Compliance Department
The compliance department is responsible for regulatory interpretation, policy coordination, evidence review, audit readiness, and internal reporting. It does not do every task itself. Instead, it coordinates tasks across teams and ensures deadlines are tracked.

### B. Operations Department
Operations handles client onboarding, account maintenance, trade support processes, record handling, reconciliations, and workflow execution that affects customer services.

### C. IT and Security Department
The IT team is responsible for platform availability, access management, cybersecurity controls, incident response, logging, backups, and technical implementation of compliance changes.

### D. Customer Support
Customer support handles queries, grievance intake, escalations, and communication follow-ups.

### E. Finance and Legal Support
This function is smaller in the demo but useful for approvals, invoice checks, disclosures, and legal review support.

### F. Product and Engineering
This team makes platform changes, user experience updates, and technical integrations when obligations affect the trading or account-opening flow.

The MVP does not need to model every department in depth, but these groups make the company feel credible.

## 12. Operating Model

SOUBH Securities operates primarily through digital channels.

### Main operating flow
1. A customer registers or logs in.
2. The customer completes KYC and account setup.
3. The operations team reviews any exceptions.
4. The IT system validates security and access rules.
5. The customer begins trading or investing.
6. Statements, alerts, and communication are generated automatically.
7. Compliance receives reports, exceptions, and issue logs.
8. The company maintains records for audit and regulatory review.

This flow matters because the compliance product should compare regulations against actual operational reality. A rule is useful only if it can be attached to a business process. SOUBH Securities is designed to have enough process depth for that.

## 17. Why This Company Was Chosen for RICE

SOUBH Securities is a good demo company because a stock broker sits directly in the center of the problem statement. It is regulated, document-heavy, process-driven, and sensitive to changing rules. It also has enough internal complexity to benefit from AI-assisted compliance automation.

This company is ideal for the MVP because:
- It is a clearly regulated business.
- It receives many rule updates.
- It needs evidence for compliance.
- It has multiple departments that must cooperate.
- It can realistically be modeled with a small amount of seed data.
- It gives the demo a believable “before and after” comparison.

In a hackathon setting, the company should not feel like a random invented entity. It should feel like a real brokerage with enough history to make a circular comparison meaningful.

## 18. Demo Readiness

SOUBH Securities is designed to be preloaded into the RICE MVP with a working baseline. That means when the application opens, the company should already contain:
- a company profile,
- a department map,
- sample policies,
- existing tasks,
- some historical evidence,
- and a current compliance state.

This is what allows the system to show “what changed” when a new SEBI circular is uploaded. Without a baseline company profile, the product would only summarize documents. With this demo company, the product can show operational impact.
